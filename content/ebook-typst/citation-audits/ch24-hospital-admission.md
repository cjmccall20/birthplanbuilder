### Citation 1: Infection Risk from Cervical Examinations Post-Rupture

**Claim (line 124):** "Every cervical check introduces a small risk of infection, and this risk goes up significantly if your water has already broken."

**✅ VERIFIED CITATION:**
- **Primary Source:** ACOG Practice Bulletin No. 188: Prelabor Rupture of Membranes. _Obstet Gynecol_. 2018;131(1):e1-e14. doi: 10.1097/aog.0000000000002663
- **Supporting Research:**
  - Lemma K, Berhane Y. Assessment of the Association of Repeated Vaginal Examination with Early Onset Neonatal Sepsis Hospital Based Follow Up Study. _Research Square_. doi: 10.21203/rs.3.rs-3918993/v1
  - Pesha R, Masenga G, Gaffur R, et al. Frequency of Digital Vaginal Examination and its Association with Chorioamnionitis and Early Neonatal Sepsis: A Prospective Cohort Study. _Research Square_. doi: 10.21203/rs.3.rs-8398440/v1
  - Dahlen H, Downe S, Duff M, Gyte G. Vaginal Examination During Normal Labor: Routine Examination or Routine Intervention? _Int J Childbirth_. 2013;3(3):142-152. doi: 10.1891/2156-5287.3.3.142

**Verification Notes:** ACOG Practice Bulletin 188 (2018, replaced by 217 in 2020) explicitly addresses infection risk with vaginal examinations after prelabor rupture of membranes. Multiple prospective cohort studies confirm increased risk of chorioamnionitis and early-onset neonatal sepsis with increased frequency of digital vaginal examinations, particularly after membrane rupture. This is well-established obstetric knowledge supported by clinical guidelines and research evidence.

**Confidence:** HIGH | **Status:** ✅ VERIFIED (DOI)

**Recommended citation text:**

### Citation 2: Intermittent Monitoring Safety for Low-Risk Labor

**Claim (line 150):** "Intermittent monitoring lets you move more freely, use the shower or tub, and change positions easily. Research shows it's safe for low-risk labors."

**✅ VERIFIED CITATION:**
- **Primary Source:** Alfirevic Z, Gyte GM, Cuthbert A, Devane D. Continuous cardiotocography (CTG) as a form of electronic fetal monitoring (EFM) for fetal assessment during labour. _Cochrane Database Syst Rev_. 2017;2:CD006066. doi: 10.1002/14651858.cd006066.pub3
- **Supporting Research:**
  - Wisner K. Intermittent Auscultation of the Fetal Heart Rate in Low-Risk Labor. _MCN Am J Matern Child Nurs_. 2024;49(4):206-212. doi: 10.1097/nmc.0000000000001030
  - Das S, Sinha T, Sarkar S. Continuous cardiotocography in active labour has no added advantage over intermittent fetal heart rate auscultation in low risk pregnancies. _Perspect J Med Sci_. 2024. doi: 10.18231/j.pjms.2024.127
  - Bayes S, Whitehead L. Cardiotocography vs. Intermittent Auscultation in Assessing Fetal Well-Being. _Am J Nurs_. 2018;118(8):51-56. doi: 10.1097/01.naj.0000534845.27761.cc

**Verification Notes:** The 2017 Cochrane review by Alfirevic et al. is the landmark systematic review comparing continuous cardiotocography with intermittent auscultation. It included 13 trials involving over 37,000 women, predominantly low-risk patients. Key findings: no significant difference in overall perinatal death rate (RR 0.86, 95% CI 0.59-1.23) between continuous CTG and intermittent auscultation. The review found reduced neonatal seizures with CTG but no difference in cerebral palsy rates and increased cesarean sections with continuous monitoring. This establishes that intermittent auscultation is safe for low-risk pregnancies with equivalent perinatal outcomes.

**Confidence:** HIGH | **Status:** ✅ VERIFIED (DOI)

**Recommended citation text:**
> Intermittent monitoring lets you move more freely, use the shower or tub, and change positions easily. Research shows it's safe for low-risk labors—large studies comparing continuous and intermittent monitoring found no difference in overall outcomes for babies.[5-8]

### Citation 3: The 3-1-1 vs 4-1-1 Rule

**Claim (line 291):** "You may have heard of the '4-1-1 rule' — contractions 4 minutes apart, lasting 1 minute, for 1 hour. This book advocates the 3-1-1 rule instead: contractions 3 minutes apart, lasting 1 minute, for 1 hour."

**⚠️ PARTIALLY VERIFIED (Clinical Guideline, Not Research-Based)**

**Web Sources Found:**
- Various childbirth education resources and doula organizations reference both the 4-1-1 and 3-1-1 rules as clinical guidelines
- Some sources also reference a 5-1-1 rule
- Multiple sources (Gwinnett OBGYN, Boston Area Doulas, Baby Chick, Women's Care Specialists) confirm these are rules of thumb used to guide when to go to hospital
- Rationale cited: arriving at 3-1-1 or 4-1-1 helps avoid arriving too early when labor may stall in the unfamiliar hospital environment

**Verification Notes:** These are clinical practice guidelines and rules of thumb taught by childbirth educators, doulas, and obstetric providers—NOT derived from specific research studies. No peer-reviewed research establishes the superiority of 3-1-1 over 4-1-1 or vice versa. The choice to recommend 3-1-1 over 4-1-1 is a clinical judgment call, not an evidence-based recommendation. The rationale (that laboring at home longer may reduce early admission and potential labor stalling) is experientially based rather than research-proven.

**Confidence:** LOW (Not research-based) | **Status:** ⚠️ PARTIALLY VERIFIED (Clinical guideline)

**Recommended approach:** The current text is appropriate as written. The book explicitly states "This book advocates" rather than "Research shows," which correctly frames this as editorial/clinical guidance rather than evidence-based recommendation. No citation needed, but could add clarifying language if desired:

**Alternative text option:**
> You may have heard of the "4-1-1 rule" — a common guideline suggesting contractions 4 minutes apart, lasting 1 minute, for 1 hour. This book advocates the 3-1-1 rule instead (contractions 3 minutes apart, lasting 1 minute, for 1 hour) to help reduce the chance of arriving too early and being sent home. These are clinical guidelines rather than research-based standards—talk with your provider about what timing makes sense for your situation.

**No citation recommended** — this is appropriately framed as guidance/preference rather than research claim.
